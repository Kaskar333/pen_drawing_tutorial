Your Journey
12-Week Beginner Timeline
You only need 15–20 minutes per day. That's it. Consistency beats long sessions. Missing one day is fine — missing a week sets you back. Treat this like brushing your teeth.
📅 Week 1 — Day 1 to 7
Making Friends With Your Pen
"This week I just get comfortable holding a pen and making marks. No pressure on quality."
  • Buy: 1 ballpoint pen (Pilot G2 or similar) + A5 sketchbook (90gsm+)
  • Day 1–2: Fill 2 pages with random lines in every direction, no goal
  • Day 3–4: Straight line drill — horizontal lines across the page (Drill #1)
  • Day 5–6: Straight line drill — vertical, then diagonal lines
  • Day 7: Draw a simple cube freehand. Don't worry if it's bad. Keep it.
📅 Week 2 — Day 8 to 14
Controlling Lines & Pressure
"This week I learn that I can control how dark and thick my lines are."
  • Pressure scale drill — lines from light to dark (Drill #2)
  • Draw 10 circles freehand per day — big, medium, small
  • Curved line drill — S-curves, arcs, waves across the page (Drill #3)
  • Buy: Micron 0.3mm + 0.5mm fineliners (or Staedtler pigment liners)
  • End of week: Draw a ball (sphere) — shade one side dark, leave other light
📅 Week 3 — Day 15 to 21
Hatching — Your First Shading Tool
"This week I learn hatching — the most important pen technique. By the end I can shade a 3D shape."
  • Hatching drill — fill boxes from light to dark using parallel lines (Drill #4)
  • Cross-hatching drill — add a second layer of lines on top (Drill #5)
  • Draw 5 boxes, shade each one differently (top lit, side lit, etc.)
  • Texture box: try hatching on the side of a cylinder you draw
  • End of week: Shade a simple mug or cup from life — just hatching, no scribbling
📅 Week 4 — Day 22 to 28
More Techniques + First Textures
"This week I add stippling and contour lines to my toolkit, and draw my first natural textures."
  • Stippling drill — shade a circle using only dots (Drill #6)
  • Contour line drill — lines that follow the shape of a rounded form (Drill #7)
  • Texture page: fill 6 squares — brick, wood, grass, water, cloud, rock (Drill #8)
  • Draw a smooth rock + a rough rock side by side — show the difference in marks
  • End of week: Draw Scene 1 — Grass Patch & Rocks (see Scenes section)
📅 Weeks 5–6 — Day 29 to 42
Trees, Plants & Organic Forms
"These two weeks I become comfortable drawing natural things — trees, leaves, grass."
  • Tree silhouette drill — draw 10 different tree shapes from memory (Drill #9)
  • Foliage texture drill — scribble technique for leafy clusters (Drill #10)
  • Grass drill — practice upward flick strokes in clusters of 5, 10, 20 blades
  • Draw a plant from life every other day (your window sill, a park, anywhere)
  • Week 5 end: Draw Scene 2 — Simple Tree at a Path
  • Week 6 end: Draw Scene 3 — Forest Edge (from the practice images)
📅 Weeks 7–8 — Day 43 to 56
Water, Skies & Atmosphere
"I learn to draw water (the most requested thing!) and skies with depth and atmosphere."
  • Water line drill — horizontal wavy lines with varying spacing (Drill #11)
  • Reflection drill — draw simple objects and mirror them in water below (Drill #12)
  • Sky drill — draw 4 sky types: clear, cloudy, stormy, sunset (Drill #13)
  • Add background/foreground to your drawings — make things feel far vs near
  • Week 7 end: Draw Scene 4 — Calm River (from practice images)
  • Week 8 end: Draw Scene 5 — River Through Forest
📅 Weeks 9–10 — Day 57 to 70
Composition & Depth
"My drawings start looking like real illustrations. I understand what makes a scene feel deep."
  • One-point perspective drill — draw a path/road leading to a horizon (Drill #14)
  • Rule of thirds — sketch 5 thumbnail compositions before drawing anything
  • Foreground/background contrast drill — same object drawn near vs far (Drill #15)
  • Try toned paper with a white gel pen for highlights
  • Week 9 end: Draw Scene 6 — Countryside Landscape
  • Week 10 end: Draw Scene 7 — Village Path (your most complex yet)
📅 Weeks 11–12 — Day 71 to 84 · GRADUATION
Full Scenes & Your Own Style
"I draw complete, finished illustrations from scratch. I have my own way of making marks."
  • Re-draw your Week 1 cube — see how far you've come
  • Work from photos — find a landscape you love and draw it
  • Spend 3+ hours on one finished piece — your most detailed yet
  • Week 11 end: Draw Scene 8 — Night Scene with Lantern
  • Week 12: Create one completely original scene from your imagination
  • 🎉 You are no longer a complete beginner. You are an artist in progress.

03
Core Practice
20 Essential Drills

Each drill targets one specific skill. Don't rush — spend multiple sessions on any drill that feels hard. A drill mastered is a skill you keep forever.

Horizontal Lines
Drill 01 · Daily · Week 1
The Straight Line Drill
Your lines will be wobbly at first. That's normal. This drill trains your arm to move in a controlled, smooth stroke.
  • Fill one page with horizontal lines spaced ~8mm apart. Try to keep them parallel.
  • Next page: vertical lines top to bottom. Move from shoulder, not wrist.
  • Next: 45° diagonals left-to-right, then right-to-left.
  • Speed variation: draw one row of slow deliberate lines, then one row of fast gestural lines.
  • Success = lines don't curve or taper at the ends. This takes days of practice.
Pressure Scale
Drill 02 · Daily · Week 2
The Pressure Scale
This teaches you that your pen can whisper (light touch) or shout (heavy pressure). This is how you show light and shadow without changing pens.
  • Draw a line that starts with almost no pressure (barely visible) and slowly increases to very heavy pressure by the end.
  • Draw the reverse — heavy to light.
  • Draw one line: light → heavy → light (shaped like a lens).
  • Try all three with curved lines and wavy lines.
  • Goal: a smooth, uninterrupted gradient with no sudden jumps.
Curves & S-Curves
Drill 03 · Daily · Week 2
Curved Line Drill
Nature is full of curves — rivers, hills, tree branches. Straight line control helps with buildings. Curved line control helps with everything organic.
  • Draw simple arcs across the page — like gentle hills.
  • S-curves: lines that change direction once, like a slow river.
  • Tight curves: small rapid waves across the page.
  • Draw spirals starting from the center and growing outward.
  • Try curves that loop back — like a tangled ribbon.
Hatching Value Scale
Drill 04 · Daily · Week 3
Hatching Value Scale
Hatching is how pen artists make shadows without rubbing or smudging. The further apart the lines = lighter. Closer together = darker. This is your most-used technique.
  • Draw a rectangle 10cm wide × 4cm tall.
  • Divide it into 5 columns: very light → light → mid → dark → very dark.
  • Fill each column with vertical hatching lines. Control darkness by spacing, not pressure.
  • The far-left column should look almost white. The far-right almost black.
  • Repeat with diagonal hatching lines at 45°, then horizontal.
Cross-Hatching
Drill 05 · Daily · Week 3
Cross-Hatching Layers
Cross-hatching builds on hatching — you add a second layer going in a different direction. Each layer darkens the area. This is how masters create deep, rich shadows.
  • Start with a hatched box (vertical lines, medium spacing).
  • Add a second layer of diagonal lines on top at 45°.
  • Add a third layer going the other diagonal. Getting dark now!
  • Try 4 layers — horizontal, vertical, and two diagonals — for maximum darkness.
  • Key rule: let each layer dry before adding the next. Never scribble — keep lines clean and parallel.
Stippling Sphere
Drill 06 · Weekly · Week 4
Stippling a Sphere
Stippling is making tiny dots to create shading. It's slow, but the results look incredible. This drill teaches you value control through dot density.
  • Draw a circle about 6cm across. Pick a light source (e.g. top-left).
  • The top-left area stays WHITE — no dots at all.
  • Moving toward the right and bottom, start placing dots spaced far apart.
  • As you approach the darkest area (opposite the light), pack dots closer and closer together.
  • Add a cast shadow below — very dense dots fading outward.
Contour Lines
Drill 07 · Weekly · Week 4
Contour Lines on Rounded Forms
Contour lines follow the 3D shape of an object, like topographic map lines. They make flat objects look rounded and 3-dimensional instantly.
  • Draw a circle. Now draw horizontal lines across it — but curve them to follow the sphere surface (they curve downward at center, like latitude lines on Earth).
  • Space lines closer near the edge (shadow), further apart at the top (light).
  • Draw a cylinder and add contour lines wrapping around it.
  • Try contour lines on a simple face profile — they follow the nose curve, cheekbones, etc.
  • Draw a rock using only contour lines — no hatching allowed for this drill.
Grass & Rock Marks
Drill 08 · Daily · Week 4
Grass Stroke & Texture Sampler
This is the exact technique in the Instagram reel you saw. Upward flick strokes make convincing grass. Master this and you can fill any landscape scene with life.
  • GRASS: Hold pen at an angle. Quick upward flick — heavier at base, lifted off at the tip. The stroke tapers naturally.
  • Cluster 5 strokes together, slightly fanning out. That's one tuft of grass.
  • Make 10 tufts in a row. Vary the height. Some bent, some straight.
  • ROCK: Draw an irregular rounded shape. Hatch the shadow side. Add a thin dark line along the base.
  • Combine: place 2 rocks, fill the ground around and between them with grass tufts.
Tree Silhouette Types
Drill 09 · Weekly · Weeks 5–6
10 Tree Silhouettes
Every landscape has trees. By learning to draw 10 different tree shapes from memory, you'll never be stuck staring at a blank page wondering how to fill a scene.
  • Pine/Fir: triangle stacked on trunk. Jagged edges, not smooth.
  • Oak: round cloud-like canopy. Trunk splits into branches before reaching canopy.
  • Willow: trunk up, then long drooping branches cascading down.
  • Palm: tall straight trunk, explosion of leaves at top only.
  • Dead/Winter tree: bare branches spread upward like spread fingers. No leaves.
  • Draw all 5 in a row on one page — small (5cm tall). Then again bigger.
Water Lines & Ripples
Drill 10 · Daily · Weeks 7–8
Water Lines Drill
Water is all about horizontal lines. Calm water has very subtle, spaced-out lines. Disturbed water has tighter, more varied wavy lines. Mastering this unlocks rivers, lakes, and rain puddles.
  • STILL WATER: Draw very faint horizontal lines across a rectangle. Lines closer at the horizon (far away), further apart in the foreground.
  • MOVING WATER: Same lines but slightly wavy and interrupted with small white gaps.
  • RIPPLES: Draw an object (rock, raindrop landing point). Extend oval rings outward from it, getting bigger and lighter.
  • REFLECTION: Draw a simple tree above a water line. Below the line, draw the tree upside down — same shape but slightly broken/wavy lines.
  • Try fast-moving water: irregular white patches surrounded by dense hatching (foam = white space, not more marks).

Technique Visual Reference
Each technique below is shown as it actually looks when drawn. Study these carefully. When you draw, ask yourself: "Which technique best describes this surface?"
Technique 1
Hatching
Parallel lines — spacing controls value
  • Far apart = light, close together = dark
  • Use for: flat shadows, walls, smooth surfaces
  • Keep all lines going the same direction
Technique 2
Cross-Hatching
Multiple hatch layers at different angles
  • Each layer adds darkness — build up gradually
  • Use for: deep shadows, rich dark areas
  • Let each layer be clean — don't scribble
Technique 3
Stippling
Value through dot density — no lines
  • Sparse dots = light; dense dots = dark
  • Use for: portraits, organic textures, precision
  • Slow but incredibly detailed results
Technique 4
Gesture / Flick Strokes
Quick directional marks for organic textures
  • Heavy at base, lift off at the tip — taper naturally
  • Use for: grass, fur, hair, rough bark, shrubs
  • Vary angles slightly for natural look

Foundation of Story Art
The 5 Skills That Separate Good from Great

Before the tutorials, understand what you're building toward. Story illustrators who work in pen need to master exactly these five things. Everything else is decoration.

🌫
Atmospheric Depth
Making distant things feel far away — through line weight, detail level, and value contrast. This is what makes a flat drawing feel like you could walk into it.
RULE: Foreground = dark + detailed. Background = light + simple.
☀️
Light & Shadow Logic
Every scene has one light source. Everything in the scene responds to it consistently — otherwise the scene feels fake. Story art lives or dies on convincing light.
RULE: Pick your sun. Never forget where it is.
🎭
Mood & Emotion
The same forest can feel peaceful, sinister, or lonely depending on how you draw it. Line quality, value range, and what you include or leave out all control emotion.
RULE: Ask "how should this scene feel?" before first mark.
👁
Visual Storytelling
Your drawing should guide the viewer's eye on a journey — foreground → middle ground → point of interest. A good illustration is a story in a single frame.
RULE: Every scene needs a focal point. One. Not three.
✍️
Personal Mark Language
The specific way you make marks — your hatching rhythm, your tree strokes, your water lines — becomes your visual voice. Readers will recognize your illustrations instantly.
RULE: Don't imitate — experiment until something feels like YOU.
T1
Master Tutorial 1
How to Draw Atmospheric Depth

This is the single biggest leap from "beginner drawing" to "story illustration." It's the difference between a flat image and a world that feels real and deep.

The Three Planes — Core Concept
FG
Foreground
  • →Thickest, darkest lines
  • →Maximum detail & texture
  • →Deepest shadows
  • →Large, fills lower frame
MG
Mid-ground
  • →Medium weight lines
  • →Moderate detail
  • →Where your story happens
  • →Usually the focal point
BG
Background
  • →Thinnest, lightest lines
  • →Almost no texture detail
  • →Silhouettes only
  • →Sometimes barely visible
Visual Example — Same Tree at 3 Distances
The Exercise: Draw the same tree three times on one page — small and faint in the top-left (background), medium with moderate detail in the center (mid-ground), large and highly detailed in the lower-right (foreground). This single exercise teaches depth better than any explanation.

Step-by-Step: Drawing a Scene with Depth

1
Thumbnail first — 2 minutes

In a tiny 4×3cm box, sketch three horizontal bands: sky (top), mid-ground action (middle), foreground edge (bottom). Decide where your focal point sits — dead center is boring, use rule of thirds (⅓ from top or side). This thumbnail is your map. Do not skip it.

2
Draw background FIRST — lightest, least detail

Counter-intuitive but critical: draw background before foreground so you don't accidentally make it too dark. Use your finest pen (0.05 or 0.1mm). For distant mountains: a single thin outline, maybe a faint hint of hatching on the shadow side. Nothing more. Distant trees: simple bumpy silhouette lines, no individual trees.

3
Mid-ground — your story lives here

This is where your characters would walk, where the mysterious cottage sits, where the ancient tree stands. Add moderate detail — individual tree shapes, basic bark texture, visible rocks. Use medium pen (0.3mm). Your focal point gets the most detail and highest contrast in this zone.

4
Foreground — dark, bold, detailed edges

Now switch to your thickest pen (0.5–0.8mm). Add foreground elements — overhanging branch, grass tufts, rocks, roots. These are often partially cut off by the frame edge, which creates immersion (the viewer feels inside the scene, not outside looking at a picture). Go dark here — rich hatching and shadows.

5
Final pass — push the contrast

Step back (literally — hold the drawing at arm's length). Does it feel deep? Usually you need to darken the foreground more and/or lighten the background. Add your deepest blacks last — the crevice of a rock, the base of a tree, a shadow under a ledge. These "anchors" make everything else look lighter by comparison.

Master Tutorial 2
Light, Shadow & Mood for Story Scenes

The same forest drawn with morning light feels hopeful. Drawn with harsh noon sun, it feels exposed. Drawn with a single beam cutting through fog, it feels mysterious. Light is emotion. This tutorial shows you how to control it.

🌅 Backlit / Golden Hour
Everything becomes a silhouette. Trees are solid black shapes. Light glows from behind. Mood: hopeful, epic, cinematic.
HOW: Draw all trees as pure black silhouettes. Leave sky almost empty — just faint lines near horizon.
🌫 Foggy / Overcast
Distant elements fade to almost nothing. Everything merges with a light grey. Mood: mysterious, melancholy, eerie.
HOW: Background = 10% opacity marks. Mid = 40%. Foreground = 90%. No hard shadows anywhere.
⚡ Dramatic Storm
Maximum darkness everywhere. One extreme light source (lightning). Mood: tense, dangerous, climactic.
HOW: Fill sky with heavy cross-hatching. One white slash for lightning. Everything else near-black.
🌿 Dappled Forest Light
Patches of white interrupt deep shadow. The light comes through gaps. Mood: peaceful, magical, inviting.
HOW: Heavy dark everywhere EXCEPT circular white zones — leave those untouched.

Master Tutorial 3
Drawing Nature's Elements in Detail

For story illustration, you need to draw forests, mountains, and rivers convincingly at different scales — from an establishing wide shot to a close-up detail. Here's how each element works.

🏔
Mountains
5 techniques
Wide shot: Layer 3 mountain ranges — each lighter and bluer than the last. Shadow faces hatched, light faces left mostly white. Pine trees in middle distance as simple triangles.
Step-by-step mountain technique:
  • 01Silhouette first — jagged peaks with one clear highest point (your focal mountain). Irregular = realistic.
  • 02Decide your light source (usually top-left). The face away from light gets hatching — diagonal lines following the slope.
  • 03Snow cap: the very top is white — no marks. Add a thin outline then leave it empty.
  • 04Rock texture mid-mountain: short, irregular marks following the slope direction. Vary pressure.
  • 05Background mountains: draw in a lighter pen or with barely any marks. Blue-grey feeling = distance.
🌲
Forests & Trees
Dense & Sparse
Close-up individual tree
Forest edge view
Looking up at canopy
The secret to a convincing forest: overlap everything. Trees partially hide each other. Branches interrupt the sky. Roots merge with the ground. No element should be fully visible with clear space around it — that's what makes a forest feel dense and real.
🌊
Rivers & Water
4 Water Types
Still / Lake
Perfectly horizontal lines. Very subtle — just hint at them. The emptier, the calmer.
Flowing River
Wavy horizontals. Lines vary in length, interrupted. Shorter gaps = faster current.
Rapids / Rocks
Broken, chaotic lines. White foam = empty paper. Lines curve around obstacles.
Waterfall
Vertical lines for fall, horizontal for pool. Splash = short curved bursts. Pool gets circular ripples.

Master Tutorial 4
Composing a Story Illustration

A story illustration doesn't just show a place — it makes the viewer feel something and want to know what happens next. Here are the composition principles that make the difference.

Rule of Thirds
Place your horizon on the ⅓ or ⅔ line — never the center. Place your focal point (the tree, the cottage, the character) at one of the 4 intersection points. Dead center is static and boring.
Leading Lines
A path, river, or row of trees that draws the eye toward your focal point. The viewer's eye follows lines naturally. Use this to guide them exactly where you want them to look — and feel compelled to follow.
Natural Framing
Use dark foreground elements (tree trunks, cave mouth, archway, rock faces) to create a frame within your frame. This focuses attention on the bright scene beyond and creates a sense of discovery — perfect for storytelling.
Focal Contrast
Make your focal point the highest contrast area in the scene — darkest darks immediately adjacent to brightest whites. Everything else stays mid-tone. The eye always goes to maximum contrast first. This is the secret to powerful story art.

What This Artist Did — Zone by Zone
① SKY
Dense, perfectly even horizontal parallel lines across the entire sky. No breaks. This creates a flat, pressured feeling — like a heavy atmosphere. Clouds are drawn with stippled contour lines that follow the rounded cloud shapes.
② MAIN MOUNTAIN
The most complex element. Each rock face/plane gets its own hatching direction — always parallel to the slope of that face. Shadow faces: heavy cross-hatching. Light faces: barely any marks, almost white. Ridge lines: a thick clean line separates each plane. Snow channels: pure white — zero marks.
③ BACKGROUND HILLS
Much lighter line weight. Minimal hatching, just enough to suggest form. No individual rock texture — just broad slope value. This creates the illusion they're far away even though they're drawn with the same pen.
④ PINE FOREST BAND
Each pine drawn as a simple triangle — but with the interior filled with short diagonal hatching strokes to suggest branch layers. Trees at the back of the band are smaller. The whole band sits as a dark mass between bright mountains and the lake.
⑤ LAKE
Heavy horizontal cross-hatching for dark water reflections. But the key: white horizontal strips left empty for sky reflections. The contrast between these dense darks and bright whites is what makes water look wet and reflective.
⑥ FOREGROUND GROUND
Organic contour scribbling — loose curved lines that follow the undulating terrain surface. Not hatching, not dots — freeform marks that suggest rocky/mossy ground. The lines literally map the 3D hills and depressions of the terrain.
How to Draw This — 8 Phase Plan
PHASE 1 · 10 MIN
Thumbnail + Horizon
Sketch 4×3cm thumb. Horizon sits at lower ⅓. Main mountain takes upper-left. Valley and lake in lower center. Mark where light comes from (top-right here).
PHASE 2 · 15 MIN
Mountain Silhouettes
Draw all mountain outlines first — main peak, secondary ridges, background hills. Use 0.5mm pen. Make peaks jagged and asymmetric — no two sides the same angle.
PHASE 3 · 30 MIN
Mountain Face Hatching
Work plane by plane. For each rock face: all lines follow the slope direction. Shadow faces get 3+ layers of cross-hatching. Snow channels = leave blank. This is the most time-consuming phase.
PHASE 4 · 20 MIN
Sky & Clouds
Rule horizontal lines across entire sky with 0.1mm pen — very close together, very light. Draw cloud outlines as rounded bumpy shapes. Inside clouds: stippled dots and contour lines, not hatching.
PHASE 5 · 25 MIN
Pine Forest Band
Draw individual pine trees as triangles — trunk line, then two angled lines. Add interior hatching. Vary heights. Pack them tightly — the negative space between trees is just as important as the trees themselves.
PHASE 6 · 20 MIN
Lake & Reflections
Draw the lake shore as an organic kidney shape. Fill with horizontal lines — heavy in dark zones, absent in bright reflection zones. Dark vertical reflection stripes mirror the trees above.
PHASE 7 · 25 MIN
Foreground Terrain
The organic scribble ground. Draw loose wavy/curved lines that follow the hills and dips of the terrain — like topographic contour lines but less precise. Denser in shadow dips, more spaced on top of mounds.
PHASE 8 · 15 MIN
Final Darks + Details
Step back. Identify the 5 darkest areas — push them darker. Add foreground pine details in the very bottom corners. Ensure the main mountain peak is the brightest point in the whole scene.

Masterwork 02 · Forest Cathedral
Ancient Forest Interior — Dappled Light
ADVANCED
Reference Illustration
Technique Breakdown
① CANOPY MASS
The canopy is drawn as overlapping dark ellipses — no individual leaves. Texture is done with short curved contour strokes following the rounded surface of each foliage mass. Sky gaps are left completely white.
② TRUNK BARK TEXTURE
Short, slightly irregular vertical strokes running down the trunk in patches. Vary pressure and spacing to create the illusion of rough, aged bark. Foreground trunks get deepest hatching — background trunks are barely suggested.
③ DAPPLED LIGHT
The most powerful technique in this piece. You don't draw light — you draw the darkness around it. Leave oval zones completely empty. Fill everything else with hatching. The white pools become sunlight by contrast.
④ FERNS & UNDERGROWTH
Each fern drawn as a central stem with short curved strokes branching off alternately. Draw the stem first, then add 6–8 leaflets per side. Ground-level plants are drawn smaller and darker than mid-height ones.
⑤ ROOT SPREAD
Roots emerge from the base of trunks as thick curved lines that narrow and fan out. They go partially underground — don't draw the full root, let it merge with the ground. This grounds the tree and makes it feel enormous.
Story Mood This Creates
"A cathedral of ancient trees. The kind of forest where you whisper. Where the light falls in columns and the roots have been here longer than memory."

Key Skill
Drawing by leaving white
Masterwork 03 · River Journey
Cascading Mountain River — Panoramic
MASTER
The Key Challenge: Water Direction
In a cascading river, water moves vertically down AND horizontally along. Your lines must show both. Vertical strokes for the waterfall drop. Horizontal curved lines for the flow in the pools between falls. The transition between these two mark directions is what makes the water feel alive.
Mountain Layering Technique
5 distinct mountain layers recede into the distance, each lighter. The trick: each layer is drawn with progressively thinner pen and less pressure. The farthest mountains are barely outlines. This creates the feeling of looking 50 miles into the distance.
Pine Tree Dark Mass Strategy
The forest on both sides forms a dark frame that makes the bright river feel even more luminous. Each pine is a simple triangle but they're packed so tightly the individual trees almost disappear into a collective mass — which is correct and realistic.

Before You Draw
Spend more time looking
than drawing.

Professional pen illustrators spend 20–30% of their total drawing time just studying the image or scene before making any marks. They identify every plane, every light direction, every technique they'll use. The drawing happens in the mind first. Your hand is just the final step.
